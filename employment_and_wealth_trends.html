Where jobs and wealth are heading towards? How the way employment and wealth are created has been changing in the last three decades¶

Analysing World Bank data from 169 countries in the last 27 years¶

Date: February, 3rd 2020

Author: David Rodrigues. Github: https://github.com/davidrodriguessp E-mail: davidrodriguessp@hotmail.com Linkedin: linkedin.com/in/davidrodrigues

1. Introduction¶

Ordinary citizens and governments alike are interested in how jobs and wealth are created. Where are the opportunities? Which are the sectors that are growing? The aim of this article is to shed light upon how employment and GDP trends have changed over the years. We have analized different sectors of the economy and found out how the importance of each of them have been growing or decreasing in terms of the jobs and wealth they create.

The data was obtained in the World Bank Global Jobs Indicators Database (JoIn), available in the website https://datacatalog.worldbank.org/dataset/global-jobs-indicators-database. It comprises more than 60 of the standardized labor supply indicators which are most commonly used in country jobs diagnostics. These cover socio-demographics, labor force status and employment type, employment composition by sector and occupation, education level completed, hours worked, and earnings.

Population and employment are shown in thousands, while GDP is presented in constant 2010 million USD. GDP per worker is calculated dividing total GDP and total employment. More details on the variables definitions can be obtained in the website https://development-data-hub-s3-public.s3.amazonaws.com/ddhfiles/160361/jobstructure_tool_demography.pdf.

Conclusions¶

The aim of this article is to shed light upon how employment and wealth dynamics have been changing in the last three decades. The analysis was based on World Bank data which includes the evolution of economic indicators from 169 countries from 1992 to 2018.

Global GDP reached around 80 trillion dollars in 2017 and the number of employed workers surpassed 3 billion in the same year. During the period analyzed both wealth and employment have continuously grown, mainly driven by the services sector.

The services sector share in terms of both wealth and employment has continuously increased over the years. It has reached more than 70% of global GDP and 50% of the employed workers. This segment growth in terms of employment happened mainly in detriment of agriculture. Agriculture has been stable in terms of the wealth it generates, however absorbing less and less employed workers. It still employed an important part of workers wordwide (1 in 3), however generating a much lower share of global GDP (less than 5%).

In the regional level, North America presented the highest share of workers employed in services (8 out of 10). This figures were similar for the U.S. and Canada. The sector has been continuously growing in the U.S. especially after 1998. The late 90's and early 2000's were marked by a revolution in the tech industry. American tech giants like Google and Amazon were created during these years. Moreover, technology started to be applied in novel ways which changed how people work and consume. For instance, ridesharing and content streaming gained momentum in the late 2000's, what might have further fostered the sector.

Europe & Central Asia and Latin America & Caribeean also stood out as highly developed in terms of the services sector. It comprised more than 60% of employed workers in these regions. More specifically, Brazil, the largest country in Latin America, presented 70% of its GDP and employment devoted to the sector.

Since 2004, the Brazilian economy has profoundly changed, being increasingly dependant upon the services sector. This happened mainly in detriment of agriculture. This latter has been decreasing since 2004 in terms of the number of employed workers dedicated to it. Nonetheless, it maintained the same share in terms of GDP. This reveals the enourmous gains in productivity that the agriculture sector achieved over the years.

In summary, global economy has faced profound changes in the last 25 years. Advances in technology and in the way people communicate have been deepply transforming how jobs and wealth are created. More and more people are performing activities related to services, while automation has continuously reduced the number of workers in agriculture and the industry. It is uncertain what the future economy will look like, but for coming years it seems that technology will continue to transform the way people make money and consume, what will probably keep fostering the growth of the services sector for years to come.
